Prompting for video

⏱ About 15 minutes🎯 Short clips, realistic expectations🎬 Brief one clip
1 Why this matters

AI video looks like magic in the demos. In real use it's powerful but fiddly: short clips, the odd weird glitch, and tools that are still rolling out. Used with the right expectations it's brilliant for a lesson hook, a scene-setter, or visual B-roll — a Roman street coming to life, a cell dividing, a stormy coastline.

This module gives you the briefing recipe and, just as importantly, an honest sense of what it can't yet do — so you don't lose an evening to it.

2 The idea

Brief it like a film director

A video prompt is an image prompt plus movement and time. Four ingredients to describe:

Scene & subject

What we see and where. "A bustling Roman market street with stalls and traders."

Motion & action

What moves and how. "Traders gesturing, people walking past, a cart rolling through."

Camera

The shot and movement. "Slow push-in", "wide static shot", "drone fly-over".

Style & length

Look, mood and duration. "Warm cinematic documentary style, ~8 seconds."

Set expectations honestly: clips are usually only a few seconds long, can take a while (or credits) to generate, and may produce glitches — wonky hands, drifting text, physics that's slightly off. Use it for atmosphere and illustration, never for anything where factual accuracy or perfect realism matters.
3 Watch it work

From "do a Roman video" to a real brief

The rough askmake a video about Roman times
The master video prompt (annotated) A bustling street in Roman Britain, market stalls with pottery and bread, togas and tunics, warm morning light, traders calling out and gesturing, citizens walking past, a wooden cart rolling slowly through the crowd, slow cinematic push-in down the centre of the street, warm, painterly documentary style, gentle ambient bustle, about 8 seconds, no on-screen text.
Scene Motion Camera Style & length
The director's eye is the lesson. "Slow push-in" and "about 8 seconds" turn a vague wish into something the tool can actually film. Keep the action simple — one or two things moving beats a chaotic crowd the model will mangle.

Then preview critically

Watch it back before you ever show a class — for glitches, anachronisms (a wristwatch in Roman Britain) and anything odd. Regenerate or simplify the action if needed.

4 Your turn

Brief one short clip (or just storyboard it)

Five minutes. If you have access to a video tool, generate a short hook clip. No access? The skill is the brief — write the full prompt and a one-line plan for how you'd use it.

Fill in & run (or storyboard)Create a short video clip: [scene and setting], [one or two things moving], [camera: slow push-in / wide static / fly-over], in a [style] style, about 8 seconds, no on-screen text. Keep the action simple and realistic.

5 🛡️ Data-safety checkpoint — video's sharper edges

No real faces, and preview everything

Video raises the stakes on a few fronts:

  • Never create video of real, identifiable people — pupils, staff or named individuals. AI "deepfakes" of real people are a serious safeguarding and data-protection issue. Keep all characters generic and fictional, and never upload a photo or clip of a child.
  • Always preview before showing pupils. Generated video can contain odd, inappropriate or inaccurate moments — watch every second first.
  • Mind copyright and music — avoid recognisable characters, branded content or copyrighted soundtracks in anything you share.
  • Most video tools are consumer products with no school data agreement — so keep them to generic, no-real-data content only.

Used for fictional atmosphere — a Roman street, a churning ocean — none of this is a problem. The line is real people and unchecked content.
