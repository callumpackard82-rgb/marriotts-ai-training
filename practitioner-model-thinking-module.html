Choosing your model & thinking level

⏱ About 14 minutes🎯 Pick the right gear for the job⚙️ One sorting task
1 Why this matters

Now you're prompting well, here's the next lever: most tools let you choose which model answers and how hard it thinks. Pick wrong and you either wait an age for a one-line email, or get a thin, rushed answer to a job that needed real care.

This is the difference between "AI is a bit hit-and-miss" and "AI nails it". Five minutes here and you'll match the engine to the task every time.

2 The idea

Two dials: which model, and how much thinking

Think of it like gears on a bike. Two dials, set them to suit the climb:

Dial 1 — the model

Light / fast models reply almost instantly and are perfect for quick, everyday jobs. Powerful / advanced models are slower but far better at complex, multi-step thinking.

Dial 2 — the thinking level

Many tools offer a "thinking" / "reasoning" mode that pauses to work through a problem step by step before answering. Great for hard tasks; overkill for simple ones.

The rule of thumb: quick, low-stakes, "just reword this" → fast model, no thinking. Complex, multi-part, "this needs to be right" → powerful model, thinking on. When unsure, start fast and step up only if the answer feels shallow.

3 Watch it work

The same week, sorted into gears

Here are six real jobs. Notice how the gear follows the complexity, not the importance:

Reword a sentence for a display⚡ Fast
Fix the tone of a quick parent email⚡ Fast
List ten retrieval questions on a topic⚡ Fast
Design a 6-week scheme of work with progression and assessment🧠 Deep + thinking
Untangle a tricky timetable / rota problem🧠 Deep + thinking
Turn raw (anonymised) assessment data into next-step groupings🧠 Deep + thinking
A fast model is plenty for pattern-work — rewording, listing, summarising. The moment a task has several moving parts that depend on each other (progression across weeks, constraints in a timetable, reasoning from data), a thinking model's step-by-step working is what stops it cutting corners. Importance alone doesn't need a bigger model — a one-line email to the Head is still just rewording.
4 Your turn

Gear-pick two real jobs

Three minutes. Think of two things on your to-do list — one quick, one meaty — and run each in the right gear in your tool of choice. Then notice the difference in depth.

A "deep + thinking" prompt to trySwitch to your most capable model with thinking/reasoning on. Design a 6-lesson sequence on [a topic I teach] for [year group], with a clear progression of skills, a hook for each lesson, and one formative check per lesson. Explain your reasoning for the order.

Where the dials live — in your tool

  • Copilot Chat offers different response styles/modes; look for a "Think Deeper" / reasoning option for harder tasks.
  • For most quick school jobs the standard mode is fine and faster.
  • Claude lets you pick the model (e.g. a faster vs a more capable one) near the message box, and toggle extended thinking for hard problems.
  • Use the capable model + thinking for planning and reasoning; the fast one for quick drafts.
  • ChatGPT has a model picker (faster vs more advanced reasoning models). Choose a reasoning model for complex, multi-step work.
  • Gemini lets you switch between a faster and a more capable "thinking" model from the selector at the top.
Current as of June 2026
Exact model names and the labels for "thinking" change every few months across all four tools. The durable skill — fast model for simple jobs, capable + thinking model for complex ones — doesn't.
5 🛡️ Data-safety checkpoint

A bigger brain isn't a safer one

Switching to a more powerful model or turning on "thinking" changes how well it answers — not whether your data is protected. The traffic-light rule from Foundations still governs everything.

Anything with real school data still goes only into Microsoft 365 Copilot Chat, signed in with your school account, green shield showing — whatever model or thinking level you pick. A clever model on a personal account is still an unapproved tool for pupil data.

One extra note: "thinking" modes often show their step-by-step working. Read it — it's a great way to catch a wrong assumption before you trust the answer.
