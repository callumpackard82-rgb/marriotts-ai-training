Prompting for images

⏱ About 15 minutes🎯 Usable classroom visuals🎨 Make one image
1 Why this matters

You typed "a picture of the water cycle" and got a soggy blob with letters that spell EVAPORTATin. The tool isn't broken — image generators need a different kind of prompt from text ones, and once you know the recipe you can make genuinely useful visuals: display art, story starters, diagrams to label, historical scenes.

Same transformation trick as text prompting — just a different set of ingredients.

2 The idea

A good image prompt has four ingredients

Vague in, vague out. Strong image prompts describe four things — an easy memory hook is S S C C:

Subject & action

What's in it and what's happening. "A Viking longship landing on a rocky shore at dawn."

Style

The look: photo, watercolour, flat cartoon, pencil sketch, oil painting, clip-art.

Composition

Framing and layout: wide landscape, close-up, bird's-eye, and where to leave space.

Constraints

Practical limits: aspect ratio, plain background, age-appropriate, room for a title.

One honest truth up front: image generators are still poor at spelling. If you need accurate labels — a diagram with the words right — generate the picture blank, then add the text yourself in PowerPoint or Canva. Don't fight the tool for typography.

See the recipe? Each ingredient removed a guess the tool would otherwise make badly. "Leave space for a title" alone saves you cropping later. And because we asked for no text, you'll add the clean title yourself.

Then iterate

Rarely perfect first time. Refine in plain language: "make it brighter", "fewer figures", "more space on the left". Image work is a conversation, not one shot.

4 Your turn

Make one visual you'd actually use

Five minutes. Think of a display or lesson image you need, and build the prompt with all four ingredients:

Fill in the blanks & run itCreate an image: [subject and what's happening], in the style of [photo / watercolour / flat cartoon / pencil sketch], [composition — wide / close-up / bird's-eye], age-appropriate for [year group], no text, leave space for a title.

5 🛡️ Data-safety checkpoint — images need extra care

Never real children — generated or uploaded

Images carry their own risks beyond the usual data rule:

  • Don't upload identifiable photos of pupils or staff to an image tool (e.g. "make a cartoon of this class photo"). That's putting biometric/personal data into an external service.
  • Don't generate images of real, identifiable people — pupils, colleagues, named individuals. Keep generated people generic and fictional.
  • Watch for bias and stereotypes. Generators can lean on clichés (who they depict as a "scientist" or a "criminal"). Sense-check before showing a class.
  • Mind copyright and likeness — avoid "in the style of [living artist]" or recognisable branded characters for anything you'll share publicly.
✗ Don't"Turn this photo of my Year 5 class into superheroes."
✓ Do"Create a generic, fictional cartoon of five diverse primary-aged superheroes."

No real child ever needs to be in the tool to get a great classroom image.
