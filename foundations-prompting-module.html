Prompting basics: ask well, get gold

⏱ About 15 minutes 🎯 Works in Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini & Copilot ✍️ One hands-on task
1 Why this matters

You've probably typed something into an AI tool, got a bland, generic answer, and quietly decided it wasn't worth the bother. The problem usually isn't the tool — it's that it was asked a vague question.

The difference between "meh" and "that just saved me forty minutes" is almost entirely in how you ask. This module teaches one simple habit for asking well. By the end you'll have turned a scruffy one-line request into a prompt that gives you a genuinely useful starter, quiz, email draft or explanation — on the first try.

2 The idea

A good prompt has four parts

A prompt is just the instruction you give the AI. Weak prompts leave the AI to guess; strong prompts tell it four things. An easy way to remember them — R C T F:

Role

Who should it pretend to be? "Act as a Year 7 history teacher…" sets the expertise and tone.

Context

What does it need to know? Year group, ability, what's been taught, how long the lesson is.

Task & constraints

Exactly what to make, and the limits: how many questions, reading age, no calculators, British spelling.

Format

How you want it back: a table, bullet points, a slide outline, with a separate answer key.

You don't have to write all four perfectly. The trick on the next page is that you let the AI write the detailed prompt for you — you just learn to spot whether these four parts made it in.
3 Watch it work

From a scruffy line to a strong prompt

Here's the move. Start with whatever rough request is in your head — don't polish it:

The rough ask make a starter activity about the Battle of Hastings for year 7

Now, instead of sending that, paste this magic instruction first. It asks the AI to upgrade your own request into a proper prompt:

The instruction that does the work Rewrite the following request as a detailed, high-quality prompt. Add the role you should play, the context you'd need from me, sensible constraints, and the format the output should take. Then ask me for anything important that's still missing. My rough request: "make a starter activity about the Battle of Hastings for Year 7."
The master prompt (annotated) Act as an experienced Key Stage 3 history teacher. I teach a mixed-ability Year 7 class of 28. They have just started a unit on the Norman Conquest and know roughly when 1066 was, but not the detail of the battle. Create a 5-minute starter activity that hooks them in, needs no printing, and uses no specialist vocabulary they won't have met yet. Include one question that stretches the most able. Give it to me as: a title, the on-board instruction for pupils, three quick questions, and a short note on the answers I should listen for. Before you write it, ask me if there's anything about timing or the class I should tell you first.
Role Context Task & constraints Format
This is the whole lesson. See how the one scruffy line grew four labelled parts? You didn't memorise a framework — the AI added them, and now you can see them.

…then do the one thing most people skip

Read it back before you run it. The AI guesses to fill gaps, and it sometimes guesses wrong. Here it assumed "mixed-ability" and "28" — if your class is a top set of 32, change it. Catching those wrong guesses is what keeps you in control instead of accepting whatever lands.

5 🛡️ Data-safety checkpoint

Keep real people out of the prompt

Prompting practice is low-risk because a good prompt rarely needs real data — "a mixed-ability Year 7 class" works just as well as a register. Make that your habit.

The standing rule: never put identifiable pupil or staff information — names, SEND/EHCP details, safeguarding notes, medical or behaviour records — into an AI tool that the school hasn't approved for it. Personal/consumer accounts must never receive real pupil data.

For anything touching real school data: use Microsoft 365 Copilot Chat signed in with your school account, and check for the green shield that confirms enterprise data protection is on. The other three tools are fine for generic, no-data tasks like the one above — but not for real data.

Anonymise first — it's a one-word habit

✗ Don't type"Write feedback for Jack Thompson, who has ADHD and a reading age of 8."
✓ Type instead"Write feedback for a Year 5 pupil, reading age ~8, who finds focus hard."

Same useful result, no personal data — and it's quicker to type, too. Even on Copilot with the green shield, still minimise: only include what the task genuinely needs.

Strong master prompt — a maths example Act as an experienced KS3 maths teacher. My set 3 Year 8 class (low-to-middle ability, 26 pupils) has just learned to add fractions with different denominators; some still struggle with finding a common denominator. Make a 10-question practice sheet that builds from easy to harder, uses no decimals, and includes two worded "real-life" questions. Keep the reading level low. Lay it out as a numbered worksheet, then a separate answer key with one line of working per question.

All four parts present, no real pupil named, ready to use. That's the bar.
