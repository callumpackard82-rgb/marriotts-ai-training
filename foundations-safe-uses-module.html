1 Why this matters

The bits of teaching that eat your evenings — drafting the parents' email, rewording a worksheet for your lower-ability group, turning a scrawl of bullet points into a coherent plan — are exactly the bits AI is best at. Used well, it hands you back time.

But "used well" in a school means "used safely". This module gives you a menu of genuinely useful jobs and a simple traffic-light you can apply in two seconds to decide what's safe to type. Get this one habit and you can use AI with confidence for the rest of your career.

2 The idea

It's a first-drafter and a thinking partner — you stay the expert

The safe, high-value pattern is always the same: AI does the heavy lifting of a first version; you bring the professional judgement to check, adapt and decide. It never replaces you — it clears the blank page. A menu of where colleagues save the most time:

Drafting & rewordingNewsletters, emails to parents, displays, letters — in the right tone.
DifferentiationTake one text or task and pitch it up or down a level for different groups.
Explaining & analogiesThree ways to explain osmosis; an analogy a Year 5 will get.
SummarisingBoil a long document or article down to the key points (check it kept the right ones).
Ideas & planningStarters, plenaries, trip activities, assembly hooks when you're stuck.
Quizzes & questionsRetrieval questions, comprehension, model answers, mark schemes.
Notice what's not on the menu: making final decisions about a child, writing the actual content of a safeguarding record, or anything where being confidently-wrong would matter. Those stay human.
3 Watch it work

Five tedious minutes, done in ten seconds

A real, everyday job — turning rough notes into a parents' newsletter blurb. Note there's no pupil data in it at all:

Rough notes you'd typeyear 4 trip to the museum, romans, kids made mosaics and clay lamps, behaviour brilliant, thanks for packed lunches, more trips next term
A usable first draft"Year 4 had a wonderful day at the museum exploring life in Roman Britain. The children worked like real craftspeople — designing their own mosaics and shaping clay oil lamps — and their behaviour throughout was a real credit to them. A big thank-you to everyone who provided packed lunches; it made the day run smoothly. We're already looking forward to more trips next term!"

Two minutes of staring at a blank page, gone. You still read it, tweak the voice, and decide — but you started from a draft, not nothing. That's the whole value of AI in a nutshell.

4 🛡️ Data-safety checkpoint — the big one

The two-second traffic light

Before you type anything into AI, glance at what you're about to paste and ask: "What colour is this?"

🟢 Green — go anywhereGeneric content with no real people in it: a worksheet on fractions, a generic parent email, an explanation of photosynthesis. Safe in any tool.
🟡 Amber — approved tool, minimise firstSchool work that needs a little real context but can be anonymised: "a Year 9 set-3 pupil who struggles with focus". Use Copilot Chat with the green shield and include only what's needed.
🔴 Red — stop, this isn't an AI taskNames with sensitive detail, SEND/EHCP records, safeguarding notes, medical info, special-category data, anything identifying a child. Don't paste it — follow your school's data protection policy and ask your DPO if unsure.

Turn red into amber by anonymising

✗ Red"Write a target sheet for Aisha Khan, EHCP for autism, who melts down during unstructured time."
✓ Amber (approved tool)"Write a target sheet for a Year 6 autistic pupil who finds unstructured time difficult."

The standing rule: personal/consumer accounts only ever see green. Real school data — even amber — goes only into Microsoft 365 Copilot Chat, signed in with your school account, green shield showing. Even then: minimise, and never put red in.

5 Your turn

Pick one job and feel the time saved

Choose a real (green) task from your week and run it. A few copy-and-go starters:

DifferentiationRewrite this paragraph at three reading levels — below, at, and above expected for Year 7 — keeping the same key facts. Here is the paragraph: [paste a generic paragraph, no pupil data]
ExplainingGive me three different ways to explain [a tricky concept I teach] to a class that's struggling with it, including one everyday analogy.
AdminTurn these rough bullet points into a warm, professional paragraph for a parents' newsletter: [your generic bullets, no names]

Run the traffic-light check first. If it's green, any tool's fine; if it strays amber, switch to Copilot with the green shield.
