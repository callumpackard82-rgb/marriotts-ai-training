What is generative AI?

⏱ About 12 minutes🎯 For the curious and the sceptical🔍 One myth-busting task
1 Why this matters

You've heard the hype ("it'll change everything") and the horror stories ("it makes things up"). Both are a bit true, and that's exactly why it's worth ten minutes to understand what's actually going on under the bonnet.

You don't need any technical background. Once you grasp the one simple idea on the next page, the tool stops feeling like magic — or like a threat — and starts feeling like what it is: a fast, fallible assistant you stay in charge of.

2 The idea

It's a very good guessing machine

Generative AI (the "generative" just means it creates text, images or code) works by predicting what comes next. Trained on a vast amount of writing, it has learned the patterns of language so well that, given your words, it can guess the most likely next words — over and over — until it has written a sentence, an email, a quiz.

That one fact explains everything else:

  • Why it's brilliant at rephrasing, summarising, drafting and explaining — that's pattern-work, and it's seen millions of examples.
  • Why it sometimes lies confidently. It's predicting plausible words, not looking up facts. When it invents a quote, a date or a "reference" that sounds right but is wrong, that's a hallucination — and it'll say it with total confidence.
The mental model: it's not Google (it isn't searching), and it isn't a calculator (it isn't reasoning from rules). It's an extremely well-read colleague who will never say "I don't know" — so you always keep your hand on the wheel.
3 Watch it work

The same tool — at its best, and at its worst

✓ Where it shines Ask: "Reword this for a parents' newsletter: 'kids did volcanoes, made models, was good.'" — and you get a warm, polished paragraph in seconds. Pattern-work it's seen a million times. Trustworthy.
✗ Where it trips Ask: "Give me a quote from the 1988 Education Reform Act about marking policy" — and it may hand you an official-sounding quote that doesn't exist. Plausible words, invented facts.

Same machine, same confidence in both. The skill isn't avoiding the tool — it's knowing which kind of task you're giving it, and checking anything factual before you rely on it.

Trust it more for shaping, drafting and rewording your own material. Trust it less — and verify — for facts, figures, quotes, references and anything you'd be embarrassed to get wrong in front of pupils or parents. You are the fact-checker; it's the first-drafter.
4 Your turn

Catch it out — on purpose

Five minutes, and the best way to build a healthy instinct: ask it about something you know inside out, then mark it.

  • 1. Open your AI tool and ask it to explain a topic you teach or know well — copy the prompt below.
  • 2. Read its answer as the expert you are. Is it all correct? Is anything subtly off, oversimplified, or simply invented?
  • 3. Then ask it: "What in that answer are you least certain about?" — and notice how it responds.
Copy this Explain [a topic you know well] to a beginner in about 150 words. Be specific and include any key facts, dates or names.

The goal isn't a perfect answer — it's to feel, first-hand, that this is a confident drafter you supervise, not an oracle.

5 🛡️ Data-safety checkpoint

What you type can be remembered

On a personal/consumer account, the things you type may be stored and even used to improve the model. So the golden rule starts here, on day one:

Never put identifiable pupil or staff information — names, SEND/EHCP details, safeguarding or medical notes — into an AI tool that the school hasn't approved for it. For this exercise you don't need any real data: pick a topic, not a person.

For anything that touches real school data, use Microsoft 365 Copilot Chat signed in with your school account and look for the green shield that confirms enterprise data protection is on. We'll go deeper on what's safe to use it for in the next module.
