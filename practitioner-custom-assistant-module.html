1 Why this matters

Back in Foundations you wrote a cracking prompt — and then, this week, you typed something very similar all over again. A custom assistant fixes that: you save your instructions once, give it a name, and from then on it's one click. A "Differentiator", a "Parents' Email Drafter", a "Prompt Polisher" — your own little team.

This is the moment AI stops being something you visit and becomes something you've built around your job. And you don't write any code.

2 The idea

An assistant is just saved instructions (+ optional knowledge)

Every tool offers the same thing under a different name — a reusable assistant you set up once. Four parts to define:

Name & purpose

What it's called and the one job it does. "Differentiator — adapts texts to three levels."

Instructions

The standing prompt: the role it plays and exactly how it should respond, every time.

Knowledge (optional)

Reference files it can draw on — a marking policy, a writing-style guide. Generic documents only.

Behaviour rules

How it interacts: ask clarifying questions first, always use British English, keep a warm tone.

The names per tool: a Custom GPT (ChatGPT), a Gem (Gemini), a Project (Claude), or an agent (Copilot). Same idea — saved instructions you reuse and can often share with colleagues.

Turn the F4 "prompt improver" into a saved assistant

Remember the magic instruction from the prompting module? Instead of pasting it every time, bake it into an assistant. Here are the instructions you'd save, annotated:

"Prompt Polisher" — set-up instructions (annotated) You are Prompt Polisher, a helper for teachers who want better prompts. When I give you a rough request, rewrite it as a detailed, high-quality prompt: add the role the AI should play, the context it needs, sensible constraints, and the output format. Always ask me for anything important that's missing before finalising. Use British English and a warm, plain tone. Never include real pupil or staff names — if I paste any, replace them with "Pupil A" and remind me.

4 Your turn

Build one assistant for a job you do weekly

Ten minutes. Pick a repeated task and build an assistant for it. A ready-to-use starter you can paste into the set-up box:

"Differentiator" starter — copy & adaptYou are Differentiator, a helper for teachers. When I paste a text or task, produce three versions — below, at, and above expected for the year group I name — keeping the same key content and a consistent format. Always ask which year group if I forget. Use British English. Never include real pupil names; if I paste any, replace them with "Pupil A" and remind me. Keep reading levels appropriate and flag any vocabulary that may need pre-teaching.

Where to build one — in your tool

  • In Copilot this is an agent. Simple declarative agents can be made in Copilot, but agents that ground on school SharePoint/Graph data need Copilot Studio, which IT must enable (it's metered, off by default).
  • Check with IT first if you want a data-connected agent — and it stays the approved tool for real data.
  • In Claude this is a Project: set shared instructions and optionally add knowledge files. Great for a writing-assistant or planning helper.
  • In ChatGPT this is a Custom GPT — there's a guided builder; paste your instructions and (optionally) upload generic reference files. Can be shared via a link.
  • In Gemini this is a Gem: name it, paste instructions, optionally add knowledge. Heads-up: uploaded files in a Gem can expire after about 48 hours — don't rely on them as permanent storage; re-add or keep the source elsewhere.
Current as of June 2026
Builders and sharing options change regularly, and Copilot agent-building in particular depends on your school's licensing. The four-part recipe — name, instructions, optional knowledge, behaviour rules — is what stays the same.
5 🛡️ Data-safety checkpoint — assistants remember

Don't feed it your class data

A custom assistant's biggest risk is its knowledge base. Anything you load in, it keeps — and anyone you share the assistant with can pull it back out.

  • Never upload class lists, mark books, reports, SEND records or anything identifying pupils into an assistant's knowledge. Load only generic documents — a public scheme of work, a style guide, a generic policy.
  • Sharing spreads the data. Before you share an assistant with colleagues, check its instructions and knowledge contain nothing personal.
  • Bake safety into the instructions — as our examples do ("never include real pupil names; replace with Pupil A").
✗ Don'tUpload "Year 9 Set 2 — names, grades, SEND notes.xlsx" as the Gem's knowledge.
✓ DoUpload the generic, public scheme of work; paste anonymised examples only.

For any assistant that genuinely needs school data, that's a Copilot/IT conversation (green shield, EDP) — not a personal-account build.
