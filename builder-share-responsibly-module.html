Build & share a tool, responsibly

⏱ About 16 minutes🎯 From "my laptop" to "my school"📝 Draft a sign-off note
1 Why this matters

A working tool on your laptop is exciting. But the moment colleagues — or pupils — use it, you've crossed a line: you may now be processing other people's data, and you're responsible for it. The last mile is where good builders are separated from risky ones.

This module gives you a deployment checklist and, crucially, the habit of getting sign-off before you go live. It's the safety capstone of the whole programme.

2 The idea

Six checks before anything goes live

Run every tool you intend to share through these. If you can't answer the question, you're not ready to deploy.

Purpose

A clear, legitimate educational reason — not "because I could".Q: what problem does this solve, for whom?

Data

Ideally none. Keep it client-side, store no personal data, require no logins.Q: does it collect, store or send any personal data? If yes — stop and rethink.

Hosting & access

Where it lives and who can reach it. A public link is public to the world.Q: who can access it, and is that the right audience?

Accessibility

Readable text, keyboard-usable, colour-contrast, works on school devices.Q: can every pupil and colleague actually use it?

Review & maintenance

Who checks it still works, and who fixes it when it breaks?Q: are you committing to maintain it, or is it disposable?

Sign-off

The non-negotiable one. IT, your DPO, and SLT as appropriate — before launch.Q: who has approved this, in writing?

The DPIA point: anything that processes personal data — especially pupils' — may need a Data Protection Impact Assessment. That's a conversation with your DPO, not a box you tick yourself. When in doubt, design the data out so you never need one.
3 Watch it work

A revision-flashcards app, done right

  • Purpose: self-quizzing for Year 11 on a topic — clear and educational. ✓
  • Data: the questions are baked into the file; pupils' answers never leave their own browser; no names, no logins, nothing stored on a server. ✓
  • Hosting: a single HTML file shared via the school's own platform (Teams/SharePoint), not a random public URL. ✓
  • Accessibility: large text, high contrast, works by keyboard, tested on a school laptop. ✓
  • Maintenance: you own it, and it's simple enough to update each year. ✓
  • Sign-off: a quick note to IT confirming it stores no data, before sharing. ✓
The design move that makes it safe: the data was engineered out. No accounts, no storage, no personal data — so there's almost nothing to go wrong. That's the goal for every classroom tool.
4 Your turn

Run a real idea through the checks

Ten minutes. Take one tool you'd like to share, work it through the six checks, and draft the short note you'd send IT/your DPO. Use this to get an honest review from your AI tool first:

Copy & fill inAct as a school data protection adviser. Here's a tool I want to share with colleagues/pupils: [describe it, including exactly what data it touches]. Review it against: purpose, data minimisation, hosting/access, accessibility, maintenance, and sign-off needs. Tell me what to change to make it data-safe, and whether a DPIA is likely needed. Then help me draft a short note to IT and our DPO.
📚 Ground it in your own policies
Your school's data protection policy, acceptable-use policy and online-safety policy are the real authorities here — read them, and talk to your DPO and IT lead early. Public guidance from the DfE and ICO on AI and data protection in schools is worth knowing too.
Current as of June 2026
Hosting options and approval routes vary by school and change over time. The six checks are durable; the specific people and platforms are local — confirm them for your setting.
5 🛡️ Data-safety checkpoint — the buck stops here

Two lines you don't cross alone

✗ Stop — get sign-off firstAnything that stores pupil data, requires logins, is pupil-facing, sends data anywhere, or goes on a public URL.
✓ Lower-riskA self-contained, no-data, no-login tool shared internally — still worth a quick IT heads-up.
  • Pupil-facing tools carry extra duties — safeguarding, age-appropriate content, and online-safety checks. A pupil using your tool is in your care.
  • You are accountable for what your tool does with people's data, even if AI wrote every line. "The AI built it" is not a defence.
  • Design the risk out — the safest tool collects no personal data at all. Reach for that first, every time.

Never let "it works" become "it's live" without the sign-off step.
